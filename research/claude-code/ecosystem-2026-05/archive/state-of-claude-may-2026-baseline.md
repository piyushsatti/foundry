# The State of Claude in May 2026: A Developer's Field Guide to Haiku 4.5, Sonnet 4.6, and Opus 4.7

## TL;DR

- **Default to Sonnet 4.6.** It is within ~1.2 SWE-Verified points of Opus 4.6, 40% cheaper, and the community-validated daily driver for roughly 80% of professional developer work — most of the gap to Opus disappears in real workflows, and Anthropic's own developer testing in Claude Code preferred Sonnet 4.6 to the older flagship Opus 4.5 in 59% of head-to-head comparisons.
- **Reach for Opus 4.7 deliberately**: GPQA-class scientific reasoning, security audits, large-scale architectural decisions, vision-heavy tasks, and long autonomous Claude Code runs where instruction-following reliability dominates raw reasoning. Use `xhigh` as the starting effort, reserve `max` for single high-stakes turns, and always pair long agentic runs with the new `task_budget` parameter to prevent the now-canonical $6,000-overnight cost blowout.
- **Use Haiku 4.5 in three places only**: sub-agents under an explicit Opus or Sonnet plan, high-volume classification/extraction/routing pipelines, and latency-bound surfaces like voice agents. Do not use it as a free-running Claude Code implementer — its tool-call malformation rate is high enough on multi-step harness work that any token savings evaporate in debugging.

## Executive Summary

As of May 2026, Anthropic's lineup has effectively become a three-tier ladder where the rungs are unusually close together. Claude **Opus 4.7** (April 16, 2026) is the unambiguous top of the commercially available stack, posting 87.6% on SWE-bench Verified and 64.3% on SWE-bench Pro, with a new `xhigh` reasoning effort level that Claude Code now defaults to. **Sonnet 4.6** (February 17, 2026) is the community-consensus daily driver: it scores 79.6% on SWE-bench Verified — within roughly a point of Opus 4.6 — at 40% lower per-token cost, and Anthropic's own developer testing inside Claude Code preferred it to Sonnet 4.5 about 70% of the time and even to the older Opus 4.5 about 59% of the time. **Haiku 4.5** (October 15, 2025) is the surprise of the lineup: at $1/$5 per million tokens it scores 73.3% on SWE-bench Verified, roughly matches the Sonnet-4-class model that was state-of-the-art only months before its release, and has reshaped how teams architect agents — Anthropic itself now uses Haiku as the Explore subagent in Claude Code. The practical reality, however, is messier than the benchmark table. Opus 4.6 went through a credibility-damaging regression episode in March 2026 in which a Senior Director at AMD published a quantitative GitHub analysis claiming a measurable quality drop; Sonnet sometimes burns more output tokens than Opus on long reasoning runs; and "max effort" Opus is a famously good way to torch a Max subscription on a problem Sonnet at medium effort would have solved. This report synthesizes those tradeoffs, with particular focus on calibrating model choice inside Claude Code.

## Key Findings

The single most important finding is that Anthropic's marketing tier names — "small/mid/flagship" — no longer match the actual capability gradient. Within the 4.x generation, the gap between Sonnet 4.6 and Opus 4.6 on SWE-bench Verified is 1.2 points; the gap between Haiku 4.5 and Sonnet 4.5 on the same benchmark is 4 points. The price ratios (5×, 3×) are dramatically wider than the capability ratios on most coding-class tasks. This is why the community's converged pattern is "barbell": route most traffic to Haiku, default to Sonnet, and reserve Opus for the narrow set of tasks where its remaining edge — primarily GPQA-style reasoning, agentic instruction-following under load, and vision — is decisive.

The second finding is that **the failure modes matter more than the benchmark scores**. Haiku's failure mode is silent malformation of tool calls in multi-step agent loops; Sonnet's is occasional confabulation under high context load; Opus's is over-engineering and burning tokens at max effort on problems that did not need it. Calibration is the entire skill.

The third finding is that **Opus 4.7 is a partial reputation reset**. The Opus 4.6 March regression episode — anchored by AMD's Stella Laurenzo opening GitHub Issue #42796 with the verbatim claim that "Claude has regressed to the point it cannot be trusted to perform complex engineering" — damaged community trust enough that the Hacker News response to the Opus 4.7 launch was conspicuously mixed-to-skeptical rather than celebratory. The benchmark numbers are excellent; the trust deficit is the harder issue, and it should shape how you architect production dependence on Anthropic.

## Details

### Claude Haiku 4.5

**Capability profile.** Haiku 4.5 is the smallest current Claude, but the version-number jump matters more than the tier name suggests. On SWE-bench Verified it scores 73.3%, only 4–6 points below Sonnet 4.5's 77.2% and well within the band where most production coding tasks succeed or fail on prompt quality rather than raw model intelligence. Anthropic's framing — that it "matches Sonnet 4 at one-third the cost and more than twice the speed" — is one of the rare marketing claims that survives independent testing. Caylent, which had been holding back on upgrading from Haiku 3.5, concluded that performance "comparable to Sonnet 4 — the model that was considered cutting-edge when it launched in August 2025 — at a price point that makes near-frontier intelligence accessible for scaled deployments" was a meaningful enough shift to justify the 25% price bump.

It is the first Haiku to include extended thinking, computer use, and Anthropic's "context awareness" training intervention, which teaches the model to pace itself across long sessions rather than quitting early — a failure mode the community calls "agentic laziness." Epoch AI, posting from @EpochAIResearch on X on October 7, 2025, noted directly: "We evaluated Claude Haiku 4.5 on several benchmarks. Even with reasoning disabled, Haiku 4.5 performs similarly or better than early lightweight reasoning models, like o1-mini."

Where it falls short is exactly where you'd expect: open-ended creative writing, deep multi-step debugging that requires holding several competing hypotheses, and tasks where instruction-following is conditional on subtle context. The Sider AI write-up — "Surviving the Quiet Genius" — describes the production gotcha well: Haiku is "the AI that shows up ten minutes early with color-coded notes," and then "without warning decides your prompt is a vibe, not a contract." The mitigation is JSON schemas, explicit citations, and routing low-confidence outputs to Sonnet.

There are also concrete tooling rough edges. RooCodeInc Issue #8702 documents Haiku 4.5 producing malformed `codebase_search` and `read_file` tool calls in multi-step analysis sequences where Sonnet 4 succeeded on the identical prompt; Continuedev Issue #8314 shows Anthropic's API returning 400 errors when Haiku 4.5 attempts to browse project files because it emits an empty text block before tool calls. These are not benchmark failures — they are agent-harness failures, and they matter disproportionately for the sub-agent workloads Haiku is most marketed for.

**Real-world use cases.** Three patterns recur across independent reports. First, high-volume classification, extraction, and routing: customer-support triage, content moderation across millions of daily events, document classification at scale. The community formulation is "run Haiku 4.5 first on all requests, then re-route low-confidence results (< 0.85) to Sonnet 4.5," which one production write-up (Markaicode) claims "cuts costs by 60–70% on typical classification pipelines." Second, voice agents and real-time chat surfaces, where the round-trip budget from end-of-user-speech to start-of-agent-speech is tight enough that anything heavier breaks the conversational feel. Third, and most consequential for Claude Code users, sub-agent execution under an Opus or Sonnet planner. Anthropic itself describes the "Plan (heavy) → Execute (many Haiku) → Verify → Review (heavy)" pattern as the recommended loop, and Claude Code now ships with Haiku 4.5 powering the Explore subagent by default.

Anthropic published an internal evaluation showing that a multi-agent system with Claude Opus 4 as the lead agent and Claude Sonnet 4 subagents outperformed single-agent Claude Opus 4 by 90.2% on their internal research eval — and the Haiku-as-subagent variant generalizes that result downward in cost. A widely circulated production write-up at mashblog.com ("Claude Haiku 4.5 vs Sonnet 4.5: Production Agent Tradeoffs," October 15, 2025) describes the unit economics directly: "With three Sonnet agents, daily inference cost was $890. Replacing the three with Haiku reduced it to $180. Response time for the full pipeline improved from 8.2 seconds to 5.1 seconds." End-to-end success dropped from 96.4% to 91.2%, but the same author adds: "Adding a retry loop brought the success rate back to 94.8% but added 1.1 seconds to failed tasks."

Augment, an early-access partner, reported in Anthropic's launch materials that "in Augment's agentic coding evaluation, [Haiku 4.5] achieves 90% of Sonnet 4.5's performance, matching much larger models." Canva reported that Haiku 4.5 "outperformed our current models on instruction-following for slide text generation, achieving 65% accuracy versus 44% from our premium tier model — that's a game-changer for our unit economics."

**Cost and speed tradeoffs.** At $1 per million input tokens and $5 per million output tokens — with prompt caching cutting repeated context by up to 90% and the Batch API offering 50% off — Haiku 4.5 is roughly 3× cheaper than Sonnet 4.6 ($3/$15) and 5× cheaper than Opus 4.7 ($5/$25). Latency typically runs 4–5× faster than Sonnet 4.5 on like-for-like tasks, and p99 stays close to p50 because the model is small enough that token generation doesn't spike under concurrent pressure.

The cost story has one community wrinkle Anthropic has not addressed cleanly: Claude Code Issue #9717 documents that on Max-plan subscriptions, "Haiku 4.5 requests are consuming my usage limits at the same rate as Sonnet 4.5 requests, negating the cost efficiency benefits of the smaller model." If you are paying API rates the savings are real; if you are on a Max-tier subscription, the math is less generous than the per-token pricing suggests. DataCamp's coverage flagged the longer-term wrinkle: within the Haiku line, prices have crept upward — Haiku 3 was about 12× cheaper than its Sonnet counterpart, while Haiku 4.5 is only ~3× cheaper than Sonnet 4.5. The class is still a value tier, but closer to "premium small model" than to "cheap fallback."

**Effort-level mapping in Claude Code.** Haiku is viable for true low-effort work — renaming variables, applying a known refactor pattern, generating boilerplate, formatting data, simple file reads, answering a focused syntactic question. The community pattern, articulated most clearly in a widely shared Medium post titled "Why Haiku Should Be Your Default Coding Model in Claude Code," is that "most of your work happens with Haiku, orchestrated by occasional Sonnet planning sessions, validated with strategic Opus reviews." That author defaults to Haiku in `/model` and invokes Sonnet only for planning and Opus only for review checkpoints.

The dissenting view, common on Hacker News and r/ClaudeAI, is that the moment Haiku is asked to do something more than executing an atomic instruction, the failure mode is silent rather than loud — it produces plausible-looking code that doesn't quite work, and you spend more time debugging the output than you would have spent letting Sonnet do it once. The Roo Code and Continuedev GitHub issues are concrete evidence of this. The practical calibration most experienced Claude Code users converge on: use Haiku only when the task is bounded and the context is small, use Haiku as a subagent under an explicit plan rather than as a free-running implementer, and do not use Haiku for debugging beyond syntax-level fixes.

**Surprises and counterintuitive findings.** The headline surprise is how often Haiku is "good enough." An r/ClaudeAI comment captured by InfoQ — "I've never built apps so fast, and it does super well. I don't even need Claude Sonnet anymore. I have been working on an app for 4 hours and I've been feeding it thousands upon thousands of lines of logs, and it had compacted the conversation like 7-8 times now" — represents a real cohort of users who quietly migrated downward. The Canva number is the more striking surprise: a smaller, cheaper model outperformed Canva's previous "premium tier" model on a structured-output task (65% vs 44%). This is the now-standard pattern: on tasks where instruction-following and schema adherence matter more than reasoning depth, the smaller model with better calibration can beat the larger model that has more degrees of freedom to "be creative." The counterintuitive caveat: Haiku is not strictly better than Sonnet 4.6 on the things you might naively assume small models are better at. Latency under load is genuinely better, but raw classification quality is a wash on hard categories, and any task where Haiku has to plan its own tool calls is risky.

### Claude Sonnet 4.6

**Capability profile.** Sonnet 4.6 is, by community acclamation, the inflection point at which Anthropic's mid-tier model stopped being a compromise and started being the default. It scores 79.6% on SWE-bench Verified (just 1.2 points behind Opus 4.6's 80.8%), 89.9% on GPQA, 89.3% on MMMLU, 97.9% on Tau2 Telecom and 91.7% on Tau2 Retail. Anthropic's own internal data showed that in Claude Code "users preferred Sonnet 4.6 over Sonnet 4.5 roughly 70% of the time" and "preferred Sonnet 4.6 to Opus 4.5, our frontier model from November, 59% of the time," with users rating it "significantly less prone to overengineering and 'laziness,' and meaningfully better at instruction following."

It supports a 1M token context window in beta, 128K max output tokens, adaptive thinking, context compaction, and computer use. The 1M context number is real but heavily contested in day-to-day use; most developers report that for genuine workflows the 200K window is plenty, and the 1M number matters only for repository-wide refactors and large document audits. The retention story is also weaker on Sonnet than Opus: Anthropic published that on the 8-needle 1M variant of MRCR v2, Opus 4.6 scores 76% while Sonnet 4.5 scored 18.5%, a gap that has narrowed but not closed in Sonnet 4.6.

Where Sonnet 4.6 is genuinely competitive with Opus 4.6: agentic coding (OSWorld-Verified 72.5% vs Opus's 72.7% is effectively a tie), single-file and bounded multi-file feature implementation, frontend code, code review, financial analysis, document comprehension (OfficeQA performance matches Opus 4.6), and adversarial robustness (Anthropic reports comparable prompt-injection resistance). Where it falls short: GPQA Diamond, the PhD-level multi-discipline science benchmark, shows a 17-point gap (74.1% vs 91.3%), which is real and consequential in practice for research-grade reasoning, novel algorithm design, and security audits requiring deep multi-step inference. ARC-AGI-2 shows roughly an 8-point gap.

The other capability footnote is that Sonnet is genuinely better at "design taste" than its position in the lineup suggests. Anthropic's launch quotes from early customers describe Sonnet 4.6 outputs as "notably more polished, with better layouts, animations, and design sensibility." This shows up in Dan Cleary's blind tower-defense test on Medium, where Sonnet 4.6 was his favorite generation by a hair over Opus 4.6.

**Real-world use cases.** The dominant use case is everything in the median band of professional developer work: bug fixes, feature implementation, writing tests, code review, single-file refactors, drafting and editing prose, building React components, implementing API endpoints, computer-use automation, agent planning. Roughly 80% of Claude Code sessions, by aggregated community report, are tasks where Sonnet's output is "indistinguishable from Opus in practice."

It is also the model that agent platforms lean on. GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Windsurf, Warp, and the various coding IDEs route a disproportionate share of traffic through Sonnet because the cost-quality ratio is dominant for assist-style use. Joe Binder, GitHub's VP of Product, said at the Sonnet 4.6 launch: "Out of the gate, Claude Sonnet 4.6 is already excelling at complex code fixes, especially when searching across large codebases is essential. For teams running agentic coding at scale, we're seeing strong resolution rates and the kind of consistency developers need." In Anthropic's own Vending-Bench Arena evaluation, Sonnet 4.6 "developed an interesting new strategy: it invested heavily in capacity for the first ten simulated months, spending significantly more than its competitors, and then pivoted sharply to focus on profitability in the final stretch," finishing ahead of every competitor — the long-horizon planning ability that translates into successful multi-step migrations in production.

**Cost and speed tradeoffs.** $3/$15 per million tokens, output ~52.8 tokens/second (vs Opus 4.6's 45.3), 40% cheaper per token than Opus on both input and output. For a team running 1,000 coding sessions per day averaging 30K output tokens each, the difference is roughly $300/day or $9,000/month over Opus.

The hidden cost story matters. Anthropic's own data shows Sonnet 4.6 used 74M output tokens in max-effort mode to run an evaluation suite, while Opus 4.6 used 58M on the same suite. Sonnet is cheaper per token but sometimes generates more tokens to reach a result on long reasoning-heavy tasks, particularly when set to high or max effort. On bounded tasks, the per-token saving wins. On long autonomous coding sessions where the model is allowed to think freely, the gap to Opus on the final bill is smaller than the headline pricing suggests.

**Effort-level mapping in Claude Code.** Sonnet 4.6 is the community consensus model for medium effort: implementing a feature against a clear spec, writing tests for an existing module, doing a defined refactor, debugging a bug whose locus is roughly known, building a non-trivial UI component, generating boilerplate that requires some judgment, code review of a focused PR. The defaults that have stabilized are `/effort medium` for most coding (the BSWEN field guide's canonical anecdote is the author setting `effort max` on a race condition only to realize after 15 minutes and half a token budget that the fix was a 3-line change available at medium); `/effort high` is the recommended bump for multi-file refactors or anything touching ≥5 files; and `max` is Opus-only and does not persist across sessions.

Dissenters who say Opus is needed at the medium tier exist, but their use cases cluster around two categories: GPQA-style reasoning tasks (security audits, novel algorithm design, scientific code) where the 17-point gap shows up immediately, and workflows where the cost of a wrong answer is high enough that the marginal price of Opus is trivially worth it (production-critical migrations, security-sensitive changes, compliance work).

**Surprises and counterintuitive findings.** The most-discussed surprise is the Tyler Folkman benchmark on his disk-map TUI, in which Sonnet 4.6 (68/100) outscored Opus 4.7 (63/100) on a real coding task graded on a seven-category scorecard. His framing was characteristic of a developing community sentiment: "Models optimize for what gets measured, not what actually ships. Sonnet 4.6 had clean vim-style navigation, color-coded output, scroll indicators showing more items above and below" — the polish that Opus 4.7's literal instruction-following sometimes strips away.

The second surprise is the inverse — Sonnet sometimes hallucinates in agent loops in ways that Opus does not. The Hacker News thread on Sonnet 4.6 includes a representative anecdote of a code-review session in which "Sonnet completely veered off the prompt it was given (review a design document) and instead came out with a verbose suggestion to do a mechanical search and replace to use this newly fabricated function name — that it even spelled incorrectly." This kind of confabulation under load is the failure mode that distinguishes Sonnet from Opus more than raw benchmark numbers do. Third, the design-taste surprise: multiple independent testers note that Sonnet 4.6 produces frontend code with better visual polish than Opus 4.6 or even 4.7 in some prompts — an output property that does not show up in any standard benchmark.

### Claude Opus 4.7

**Capability profile.** Opus 4.7 is the most capable generally available Claude model as of May 2026 — *generally available* doing the work, because the unreleased Claude Mythos Preview leads it on most benchmarks (SWE-bench Pro 77.8% vs 64.3%, SWE-bench Verified 93.9% vs 87.6%, Terminal-Bench 82.0% vs 69.4%, GPQA Diamond 94.6% vs 94.2%). Mythos is invitation-only research preview within Project Glasswing.

Among models you can actually use, Opus 4.7 sits at or near the top across the metrics that matter for production coding agents: 87.6% on SWE-bench Verified (up from 80.8% on Opus 4.6), 64.3% on SWE-bench Pro (up from 53.4%), 70% on CursorBench (up from 58%), 77.3% on MCP-Atlas for multi-tool agentic work, 79.5% on visual navigation without tools (up from 57.7%), and 94.2% on GPQA Diamond. SWE-bench Verified leadership briefly passed to GPT-5.5 on April 23 at 88.7%, but Opus 4.7 retains the SWE-bench Pro and MCP-Atlas leads.

The signature capability change is stricter instruction-following. Where prior Claudes would interpret instructions "loosely" and fill in gaps with model judgment, Opus 4.7 takes instructions literally: tell it "respond in JSON," it returns JSON and nothing else; tell it "write exactly 3 functions," it writes 3 even if 4 would be more elegant. Anthropic's launch notes explicitly warned developers to re-tune prompts written for earlier models. This is genuinely better for production systems but breaks prompts that relied on model judgment.

The second material change is the vision upgrade: image resolution increased from 1568px / 1.15MP to 2576px / 3.75MP (roughly 3× more pixels). One early-access partner testing computer vision for autonomous penetration testing reported visual acuity jumping from 54.5% on Opus 4.6 to 98.5% on Opus 4.7 — a number cited in Anthropic's launch materials that should be treated as a vendor-supplied ceiling until independently replicated.

The third change is the xhigh effort level, which sits between high and max and is now the Claude Code default for all subscriber plans on Opus 4.7. Anthropic's own data shows xhigh approaches 75% on coding tasks while max pushes higher at significantly more token cost. As Anthony Maio's effort-level guide notes, the whole capability ladder shifted up in 4.7: "Low-effort 4.7 is roughly equivalent to medium-effort 4.6."

Where Opus 4.7 falls short or regresses: the new tokenizer encodes text 1.0–1.35× less efficiently than Opus 4.6's tokenizer depending on content type, so the same input may consume more tokens — a stealth cost increase. An independent assessment by MindStudio found Opus 4.7 "regresses on web research" relative to Opus 4.6, with BrowseComp being the one benchmark in the launch table where Opus 4.7 underperforms a competitor (Gemini leads). Temperature, top_p, and top_k controls now return a 400 error if set to anything but defaults; extended thinking budgets are deprecated in favor of adaptive thinking only. Both are migration tasks, not abstract warnings.

**Real-world use cases.** The use cases where developers explicitly reach for Opus 4.7 cluster tightly. Architectural decisions, large-scale refactors, complex multi-file changes; security audits, vulnerability research, and security-sensitive code review (with the caveat that the new Cyber Verification Program now gates some offensive-security use cases); long autonomous agent runs where instruction-following matters more than raw reasoning. Intuit reported Opus 4.7 "catching its own logical faults during the planning phase and accelerating execution." Vercel observed it "doing proofs on systems code before starting work, which is new behavior." Rakuten reported 3× more production tasks resolved compared to Opus 4.6.

Finance, legal, and document-heavy enterprise work is the other heartland: Opus 4.7 is state-of-the-art on GDPval-AA (economically valuable knowledge work) and posts a 23-point improvement over Sonnet 4.5 on Anthropic's Real-World Finance evaluation. Vision-heavy workflows (design review, dense diagram interpretation, screenshot analysis in computer-use loops) are where the resolution upgrade is non-marginal. And multi-agent orchestration — especially the "Opus orchestrator with Sonnet/Haiku workers" pattern — is where Opus 4.7's literal instruction-following pays compounding dividends across tool calls.

**Cost and speed tradeoffs.** $5/$25 per million tokens, ~45.3 tokens/second output. The "$720/day savings at scale" framing from various blog posts is real: for 1,000 daily requests on a typical 10,000-token coding task, Sonnet costs ~$180/day versus Opus's ~$900/day. Whether that's worth it depends entirely on whether the Opus quality delta translates into shipped work for your team — for most workloads it does not.

The cost-blowout stories are well-documented and real. Tomasz Tunguz publicly posted on X that "by Monday lunch, I had burned through my Claude code credits. I'd been warned; damn the budget, full prompting ahead. I typed `ultrathink` to solve a particularly challenging coding problem... When that still couldn't solve the issue, I summoned Opus, the biggest & most expensive model, to solve it." Uber's CTO Praveen Neppalli Naga confirmed to The Information (reported via Forbes) that he "burned through roughly $1,200 during a single two-hour demo session" and that Claude Code adoption inside Uber jumped from 32% to 84% of the 5,000-engineer organization, exhausting the company's full 2026 AI budget in four months. Per-engineer monthly costs averaged $150–$250, with heavy users in the $500–$2,000 range; Naga said the company is "back to the drawing board" on AI budgeting.

The most striking single-incident story is MakeUseOf's May 22, 2026 reporting by Oluwademilade Afolabi on a Reddit user who left Claude Code looping a 30-minute "check for updates" command overnight and woke up to a roughly $6,000 bill. The root cause was a quiet Anthropic change cutting the prompt cache TTL from one hour to five minutes, which meant each 30-minute loop rebuilt an 800,000-token context window from scratch — repeating 48 times per day at full cache-write rates rather than the cached pricing the developer had been assuming.

The new `task_budget` API parameter (public beta on Opus 4.7, header `task-budgets-2026-03-13`) directly addresses this: it sets a hard token ceiling across an entire agentic loop, with the model receiving a running countdown and prioritizing work as the budget shrinks. For teams running overnight autonomous agents this is the single most consequential operational feature in the 4.7 release.

**Effort-level mapping in Claude Code.** The Anthropic-recommended starting point for Opus 4.7 in Claude Code is xhigh (the new default) for coding and agentic work, with max reserved for specific failure modes you've verified it solves. The community-converged framework from Anthony Maio's well-circulated effort-level guide: `low` for quick lookups and mechanical edits; `medium` for simple coding; `high` for multi-file refactors and cross-file debugging; `xhigh` for long autonomous coding sessions, repeated tool calling, and exploratory work; `max` reserved for architecture decisions, subtle race conditions, and security review with "no constraint on token spending — don't blanket-set this."

The community rule that overrides everything: "a model on low with great context beats the same model on max with bad context." If you're reaching for max on tasks that should be doable at high, the fix is upstream — better CLAUDE.md, atomic task scoping, less ambiguity. "Garbage in at max effort is just garbage out with a bigger bill."

The other widely adopted pattern is `opusplan` — Opus plans, Sonnet executes — which uses Claude Code's hybrid mode to invoke Opus only during plan-mode and automatically drop to Sonnet for code generation. This captures most of Opus's reasoning value at roughly Sonnet's cost and is now the recommended default for refactoring sessions, feature planning, and any workflow that uses plan-mode regularly.

**Surprises and counterintuitive findings.** The defining surprise of early 2026 was that Opus 4.6 regressed in the field. Stella Laurenzo, Senior Director of AI at AMD, opened GitHub Issue #42796 on April 2, 2026 with a quantitative analysis of 17,871 thinking blocks and 234,760 tool calls across 6,852 Claude Code sessions, concluding bluntly that "Claude has regressed to the point it cannot be trusted to perform complex engineering. Ignores instructions. Claims 'simplest fixes' that are incorrect. Does the opposite of requested activities. Claims completion against instructions… Claude should behave like it did in January." Laurenzo added that "every senior engineer on my team has reported similar experiences" and that AMD had switched to another provider for the critical work, with the explanatory note: "All I will add is that 6 months ago, Claude stood alone in terms of reasoning quality and execution. But the others need to be watched and evaluated very carefully. Anthropic is far from alone at the capability tier that Opus previously occupied."

Boris Cherny, head of Claude Code, replied that the redaction header Laurenzo blamed was a UI-only change and "does not impact thinking itself," but community sentiment did not recover before the 4.7 launch. The top Hacker News comment on the Opus 4.7 launch (item 47793411, April 16, 2026) was characteristic, posted by user buildbot: "Too late, personally after how bad 4.6 was the past week I was pushed to codex, which seems to mostly work at the same level from day to day. Just last night I was trying to get 4.6 to lookup how to do some simple tensor parallel work, and the agent used 0 web fetches and just hallucinated 17K very wrong tokens." Another top commenter, TIPSIO, quipped: "Quick everyone to your side projects. We have ~3 days of un-nerfed agentic coding again." A third, sleazebreeze: "It's an abusive relationship at this point." User cmrdporcupine wrote: "I've been getting better results out of codex on and off for months. It's more 'careful' and systematic in its thinking… Anthropic has pissed in the well too much."

The reputational point is worth taking seriously: Opus 4.7 is in part a reset, and the credibility damage from the 4.6 episode is real enough that many shops have diversified their stack to Codex or Gemini for parts of their workflow. The benchmark numbers are excellent; the trust deficit is the harder issue.

The other surprise is the inversion in some niche benchmarks. Tyler Folkman's blind real-world coding test had Sonnet 4.6 outscoring Opus 4.7 (68/100 vs 63/100), and at least one production team reported Sonnet 4.6 producing better dashboard/data-rich interface code than Opus 4.7 in early A/B testing — despite Anthropic's own marketing claiming Opus 4.7 is "the best model in the world for building dashboards and data-rich interfaces."

### Synthesis: A Decision Framework for Model Selection

**The signal hierarchy.** When choosing a model, the signals that should drive your decision, in priority order, are: (1) Task class — is this a classification/extraction/routing task (Haiku), a default coding/feature/refactor task (Sonnet), or a reasoning-heavy / cross-file / security-sensitive task (Opus)? (2) Cost of being wrong — if a wrong answer is cheap, use the smaller model; if it's expensive, use Opus and turn up effort. (3) Whether the model is also calling tools — tool-use reliability and instruction-following matter much more than raw reasoning depth in agent loops, which is exactly where Opus 4.7's strict-literal interpretation earns its premium and where Haiku's tool-call malformation rate hurts disproportionately. (4) Volume and latency — at >1,000 daily requests the cost gradient compounds; at <800ms required round-trip, Haiku is often the only choice. (5) Whether you need the 1M context window — most workflows do not.

**The decision matrix.**

| Effort level | Task examples | General API recommendation | **Claude Code recommendation** | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Low** | Renaming variables, syntax fixes, formatting, regenerating boilerplate, simple file reads, classifying or extracting from structured text, FAQ answering | **Haiku 4.5** | **Sonnet 4.6 at `/effort low`** is the community default; use Haiku 4.5 explicitly only when you've validated the prompt is bounded and atomic | Haiku's failure mode on Claude Code's tool-heavy harness is silent malformation of tool calls; Sonnet at low effort is barely more expensive in practice |
| **Medium** | Implementing a feature against a spec, writing tests, single-file refactor, bug fix where the locus is known, building a React component or API endpoint, code review of a focused PR | **Sonnet 4.6** | **Sonnet 4.6 at `/effort medium`** (the default) | This is the band where Sonnet 4.6's 1.2-point SWE-Verified gap to Opus is genuinely irrelevant |

**Edge cases and exceptions.** Frontend / design-taste work: Sonnet 4.6 sometimes produces more polished UI than Opus 4.7; A/B test rather than assuming up-tier is better. Workflows tuned on Opus 4.6: the new tokenizer and stricter instruction-following in Opus 4.7 will break prompts that relied on model judgment, so re-tune before upgrading. High-volume classification with a long tail: the community cascade pattern — Haiku first, route confidence <0.85 to Sonnet — beats either model alone in cost-quality terms. Voice agents: latency budget alone forces Haiku; Sonnet at `/effort low` is too slow. Workflows hitting rate limits: Haiku has higher headroom on most subscription tiers, though Claude Code Issue #9717 documents this isn't always reflected correctly in Max-plan usage accounting. Anything needing 1M context: Sonnet 4.6 now has 1M context at standard pricing (the long-context premium was removed alongside the 4.6 release), so use Sonnet for repo-scale reads unless you also need Opus-class reasoning over that context.

## Recommendations

**For solo developers and small teams just adopting Claude Code:** Default to Sonnet 4.6 at `/effort medium` for everything for the first two weeks. Do not optimize for cost until you have a sense of which tasks Sonnet handles well and which it struggles with. Once you have that baseline, introduce two patterns: invoke `opusplan` mode for planning-heavy sessions, and start dropping to Haiku for genuinely bounded tasks like regenerating boilerplate or running through a list of simple edits. Do not put Haiku in your free-running implementer slot.

**For teams with $1,000+/month in Claude Code spend:** Implement the "barbell" architecture. Use Opus 4.7 only as an orchestrator/planner (xhigh effort), Sonnet 4.6 as your default executor (medium effort), and Haiku 4.5 as parallel workers under explicit plans. Anthropic's 90.2% internal-research-eval lift from a multi-agent system over single-agent Opus is the validation. Set `task_budget` on every autonomous loop and audit your `/usage` weekly. The mashblog production case (3 Sonnet sub-agents at $890/day rewritten to 3 Haiku workers at $180/day for a ~5% drop in end-to-end success, recoverable to 94.8% with a retry loop) is the canonical economics to benchmark against.

**For teams making production deployment decisions:** Pin model IDs (not aliases) in your code. Run your own benchmarks on your real codebase before upgrading from 4.6 to 4.7, particularly if you have prompts relying on model judgment to fill gaps — the Opus 4.7 literal-instruction behavior will break them. Build in a manual fallback path that does not assume Anthropic API is up; Claude Code Issue #35269 documented 14+ hours of silent Haiku-only fallback on Max plans during one incident. Diversify your stack to at least one non-Anthropic provider (Codex or Gemini 3.1 Pro) for business continuity — the Stella Laurenzo / AMD episode and the resulting Hacker News sentiment make this a prudent risk-management posture rather than a competitive consideration.

**Benchmarks that would change these recommendations:** (a) If Sonnet 4.7 / 4.8 closes the GPQA gap to Opus, push Sonnet harder into the "high — research/science/security" tier. (b) If a future Haiku ships with a measurably lower tool-call malformation rate, broaden its Claude Code use beyond bounded sub-agent execution. (c) If Anthropic's `task_budget` and CLAUDE_CODE auto-fallback semantics become reliable enough to prevent overnight cost blowouts, the case for letting Opus run autonomously gets stronger; until then, treat long Opus runs as supervised. (d) If Mythos becomes generally available at predictable pricing, the entire matrix shifts — Mythos's SWE-bench Pro 77.8% would make Opus 4.7 the new "Sonnet" and Mythos the new "Opus."

## Caveats

The Opus 4.6 regression episode of March–April 2026 is the most important community datapoint to internalize. Whatever the technical cause, a senior AI director at AMD published a quantitative analysis claiming a measurable quality regression; Anthropic's response was that the change was UI-only; and community sentiment did not fully recover before the 4.7 launch. This means: pin model IDs (the Claude 4.6 generation introduced dateless pinned-snapshot IDs); run your own benchmarks (Tyler Folkman's "I tested Opus 4.7 against Sonnet 4.6 — the newer model lost" experience is replicable enough that you should not assume headline benchmark numbers translate to your workflow); and be aware of silent fallback (Claude Code Issue #35269 documented Opus and Sonnet returning 400 errors on a Max plan for 14+ hours while only Haiku 4.5 was served, with Claude Code's VS Code extension showing no indication of the downgrade).

Anthropic's published benchmarks should be treated as a ceiling rather than ground truth. Independent testing consistently shows tighter margins than vendor numbers, and several launch claims (tokenizer parity with predecessors, web-research performance, partner-reported 98.5% visual acuity, the "agentic uplift" framing) have not been independently verified at the time of writing. The MindStudio analysis showing Opus 4.7 regressed on web research relative to 4.6 is the kind of detail Anthropic does not lead with in launch materials.

Many of the "developer commentary" quotes circulating online — particularly attributed to anonymous "Reddit users" in aggregator blogs — could not be independently verified to their original source. The named, traceable quotes used in this report (Stella Laurenzo on GitHub #42796, the Hacker News commenters on item 47793411, Boris Cherny's responses, Tomasz Tunguz on X, Tyler Folkman's Substack benchmark, Anthropic's customer quotes from Augment / Canva / GitHub / Vercel / Intuit / Rakuten) should be weighted more heavily than the diffuse "community consensus" claims, which represent aggregated sentiment rather than verified attribution.

Sonnet 4.8 has been rumored via Vertex AI infrastructure leaks, GPT-5.5 has already displaced Opus 4.7 from the top SWE-bench Verified slot at 88.7%, and the Mythos research preview hangs over the entire lineup as the next capability inflection. The matrix above will need to be rebuilt within months. Treat it as a snapshot, not a destination.
