The intent (your words)

Intent 1 — execution reliability at scale
"I could plan + interpret simple features reliably, but could not trust an end-to-end ship on a massive plan file — too much context, needed too much human validation."
— user, 2026-05-24
Intent 2 — resource management offloaded from human
"Claude should decide what each phase needs — what model and what verifier — not me."
— user, 2026-05-24

These are two distinct but related frustrations. The first is about plan size exceeding reliable execution capacity — context overflows mid-run; human validation becomes too frequent to feel like assistance. The second is about cognitive load on the operator — having to decide "what model do I dispatch this with? what verifier should run?" per phase puts work back on the human that the skill should absorb.

Both intents argue toward the same shape: decompose work into pieces that each fit in one reliable execution, and let the skill itself auto-allocate the resources (model, verifier, depth) for each piece based on its kind. The human's job is to define the work; the skill's job is to figure out how to execute it.

Whether the current skill's specific design (DAG of phases, contracts as producer/consumer documents, cascade engine, 5-stage gating, 9-row model allocation table that the human must consult manually) is the right shape for these intents — or whether it's overshot in one direction and undershot in another — is what the rest of this document asks.

The user-facing surface

What you give it

  • A spec with: what, why, metrics, scope/non-goals, constraints, existing context pointers
  • Project must touch ≥2 components AND be multi-hour+ work — else skill refuses and recommends superpowers:writing-plans
  • You must be on Opus (Sonnet warns, Haiku refused)

What you get back

  • A work-dir at ~/.claude/plan-orchestrator-runs/<slug>/<ts>/
  • A DAG manifest of phases (dag.md) with explicit contracts at phase boundaries
  • Per-phase plan.md files (Claude-readable, TDD-shaped)
  • An HTML review at gate boundaries (make-review.py)
  • Progress state in a sibling MCP server (progress-tracker)

Cost

The skill itself estimates $20–$100 per invocation: 30–60 Opus dispatches + 10–20 Sonnet + ~50 Haiku checks. Half a day to a day of wall-clock when actually run.

The tension

A user with a manifold spec asks: "Do I also need plan-orchestrator? Couldn't manifold's contract layer just BE the phase contracts?" The honest answer today is they're parallel solutions with overlapping vocabulary, and neither was designed with the other in mind. The overlap is real:

  • plan-orchestrator's DAG of phase+contract nodes is structurally a 2-layer manifold spec (phases as capabilities, typed contracts as the contracts layer).
  • manifold's "intent / capabilities / contracts / realizations" decomposition matches plan-orchestrator's "spec / phases / phase-contracts / shipped code."
  • Both want to verify their respective DAGs. Manifold has a generalized validation engine; plan-orchestrator has 8 ad-hoc scripts. The scripts could be specific verdict mechanisms in manifold's framework.

Three possible futures

A. Keep separate; build the bridge

Stay two skills. Wire delegate_to: plan-orchestrator end-to-end: manifold contract nodes can dispatch a plan-orchestrator run; the run's output lands back as a verdict + new realizations. Honest about the duplication; pragmatic about scope.

B. Subsume plan-orchestrator into manifold

plan-orchestrator becomes a manifold mode (`manifold run <contract>`) that creates a temporary 2-layer sub-spec under the contract, dispatches phases with auto-allocation, lands realizations. One skill, one vocabulary. Significant refactor; biggest scope-cut win.

C. Split plan-orchestrator into two skills

One skill produces the DAG (output: a manifold-format sub-spec). A separate skill runs phases with auto-allocation (intent 2). Each is simpler than today's plan-orchestrator. The split aligns with where the audits independently landed.

The alignment questions

1 Does the 5-stage shape match your actual frustration?
Intent 1: massive plan files break end-to-end ship. The skill's response: decompose into a DAG with typed contracts at boundaries. Is "DAG with typed contracts" the right shape, or would something simpler — "a list of phases small enough each fits in one execution, with a one-page handoff doc between" — solve the actual pain without the 72-decision design?
2 What's the auto-allocation API in your head?
Intent 2 is about offloading "what model / what verifier per phase" from you to the skill. The simplest version: each phase declares its kind ("planner-style decomposition" / "implementation" / "edge review" / "skeptic" / "claim check"), and the skill maps kind → model + thinking depth + verifier set. The current 9-row table is the heuristic; just needs to be executable. Is that the shape you want, or something more dynamic (the skill infers phase kind from the spec)?
3 Which of the 11 mechanisms (cards above) would you actually use?
Honestly rank: ORIENT, work-dir layout, structural verifiers, HTML review, cascade engine, phase-complete gate, mechanical dispatcher, multi-model allocation, auto-verifier selection, Haiku alignment loop. Of these, which would you actually use? Which would you skip? (The two intent-2 cards probably move from "potentially skip" to "must build" given your message.)
4 Is the $20–$100 per invocation gate the right size?
A skill that costs $20+ per use needs the projects it runs on to be large enough that the cost is worth it. "Massive plan files" was your phrase, but where's the threshold? At what project size does the ceremony actually pay off vs. doing it conversationally with Claude + just being careful?
5 Has it actually been used yet, on a real project?
The audits both flag that the design has been iterated through reasoning + synthetic test beds, not field use. Before deciding what to keep vs cut, an honest answer here would calibrate everything. If "not yet," then audit 2's recommendation — ship a small core, let real usage drive what gets added back — is probably right.
6 If we rename, does "contract-dag" fit?
Both audits independently proposed "contract-dag." With intent 2 made explicit, an alternative shape emerges: "phase-conductor" or "auto-allocator" — names that highlight the skill is doing the dispatch + resource management, not just specifying a structure. Or maybe two skills: one that produces the DAG (a spec output), one that runs phases with auto-allocation (a dispatcher).
7 Manifold + plan-orchestrator: A, B, or C?
Given the vocabulary collision and structural overlap above: should they (A) stay separate with an explicit bridge wired, (B) merge into manifold as a "run mode," or (C) split plan-orchestrator into two simpler skills, one of which outputs manifold-format spec? The answer here changes everything downstream — a rename and a cut list look different in each world.
