§1 Origin: who, when, where, why

KAOS stands for Knowledge Acquisition in autOmated Specification. It is a methodology for goal-oriented requirements engineering. It was founded in the early 1990s by Anne Dardenne, Axel van Lamsweerde, and Stephen Fickas. Van Lamsweerde is the central figure; nearly every major KAOS paper from 1993 onward carries his name. The work was done at the Université catholique de Louvain in Belgium.

First formal paper: Dardenne, van Lamsweerde & Fickas, 1993 "AND/OR DAG" definition: Darimont & van Lamsweerde, FSE-4, 1996 Canonical textbook: van Lamsweerde, Wiley, 2009

The problem KAOS was invented to solve

In the late 1980s, the dominant practice in industrial software requirements was the shall list: a flat enumeration of "the system shall do X." This produced documents that were long, hard to verify, hard to detect omissions in, and disconnected from why anything was being built. The famous failure modes of the era — the London Ambulance Service dispatch failure (1992), the Ariane 5 launch loss (1996), the Therac-25 radiation overdoses (1985–87) — were all post-mortem'd as requirements failures, not coding bugs.

KAOS was an answer to four specific weaknesses of the shall-list:

  1. Intent was lost. A shall-statement tells you what but not why. KAOS started with goals (intentions) at the top and refined them downward.
  2. Completeness was undetectable. If you missed a requirement, the document didn't visibly have a hole. KAOS's coverage rule (every non-leaf goal must have ≥1 child) makes omissions structurally visible.
  3. Conflicts were invisible. Two shall-statements could contradict each other across a 300-page document. KAOS formalized goal-conflict detection.
  4. Threats were undocumented. Things that prevent a goal from holding (KAOS calls them obstacles) were nowhere in the shall-list. KAOS made obstacle analysis a first-class step.

It was invented for safety-critical software — air traffic control, medical devices, train control, banking — where flat shall-lists had measurably failed. But the formalism turned out to generalize.

§2 The core idea: goals, refinement, satisfaction

A KAOS spec is built from goals. A goal is something to be achieved, maintained, avoided, or ceased — a desired property of the system over time.

The structural primitive is AND-refinement (with a sibling, OR-refinement):

Manifold currently uses ONLY AND-refinement. KAOS's OR-refinement is the formal home of "we can satisfy this goal in any of these alternative ways" — useful when you have multiple acceptable designs. (Manifold's current model has no first-class concept for this; it would just be a fork in the tree.)

The tree-vs-DAG fact, stated formally

This is the line of primary text that closes the question we were debating:

"The goal refinement structure for a given system can be represented by an AND/OR directed acyclic graph." — Darimont & van Lamsweerde, "Formal Refinement Patterns for Goal-Driven Requirements Elaboration," FSE-4 (ACM, 1996), §2

Not a tree. Not a forest. A directed acyclic graph. A goal may have multiple parents (it contributes to multiple higher-level intentions). This isn't a workaround in KAOS — it's part of the formalism's definition.

The exact pattern our research-paper stress test surfaced. In KAOS this is unremarkable; in a tree-only formalism it is a "structural lie."

§3 The formal object: an AND/OR DAG over LTL

A KAOS specification is a 6-tuple, but for our purposes only the first two parts matter:

The KAOS model, simplified

  1. Goals — a set of nodes G, each expressed in linear temporal logic (LTL) with one of four patterns: Achieve (eventually true), Maintain (always true), Avoid (never true), Cease (becomes false).
  2. Refinements — a set of edges R ⊆ G × P(G), where each edge connects a parent goal to a set of child goals AND-refining or OR-refining it. The structure (G, R) is an AND/OR DAG.
  3. Agents — humans or system components who are responsible for goals at the boundary.
  4. Operations — what an agent does to satisfy a leaf goal.
  5. Objects — domain entities the goals reason about.
  6. Obstacles — conditions that prevent a goal from holding; analyzed and refined the same way as goals.

Soundness — when is the AND-refinement formally correct?

AND-refinement is proven sound when ⋀ᵢ subgoalᵢ ⊨ parent. — van Lamsweerde, Requirements Engineering, Wiley 2009, Ch. 7 §7.1.2

That is: if you can show that the conjunction of all children logically entails the parent, the refinement is sound. KAOS publishes a catalog of refinement patterns — proven-sound decomposition templates — so that you don't have to prove soundness from scratch for common cases (milestone-driven, case-driven, guard-introduction, etc.).

Manifold doesn't currently require formal proof of soundness — its verdict mechanisms are operational (shell out, LLM judge, human signoff). But the shape of the requirement is the same: the children, taken together, must be sufficient for the parent.

§4 Why KAOS and not the other four traditions

The deep research compared four formal traditions for the tree-vs-DAG decision. Here is the short version of why each alternative falls short of KAOS as the formal home for manifold.

TraditionWhat it formalizesWhere it falls short for manifold
Chomsky / generative grammar Derivation of strings from a grammar; phrase structure. Wrong domain. It models language production, not intent decomposition. The "tree" you get is a parse tree, which is about syntactic structure of utterances. Dependency grammar (an alternative within the tradition) explicitly forbids multi-parent — the opposite of what manifold needs.
Refinement calculus (Back & von Wright, Dijkstra) A partial-order relation ⊑ between program specifications: "S₂ refines S₁" means S₂ is more concrete and still correct. Wrong shape. It relates whole specs to whole specs via ⊑; it doesn't natively decompose ONE spec into sub-specs. The Hasse diagram of refinements IS a DAG, but the DAG is between candidate implementations, not between a goal and its parts. Closer to "version graph" than "decomposition graph."
AND-OR graphs / HTN (Martelli & Montanari; Erol, Hendler & Nau) The algorithmic structure of problem-reduction: a parent problem reduces to a conjunction (AND) or disjunction (OR) of subproblems. Right shape, but missing the layered intent. AND-OR graphs are flat in altitude; KAOS adds the notion of strategic vs. operational levels (you start with business intent, you end with operational specs). Manifold's layered abstraction is closer to KAOS.
i* / iStar 2.0 (Yu) Goal-oriented modeling with explicit modeling of actors, dependencies between actors, and contributions. Too much machinery. iStar's primitives include actor boundaries, dependency links (one actor needs another's resource), and qualitative contribution labels (Make/Help/Hurt/Break). Manifold doesn't model "who depends on whom" at the spec level. Useful if/when manifold grows that, but premature.
NFR / Softgoal Interdependency Graph (Chung, Nixon, Yu, Mylopoulos) Decomposition with qualitative aggregation (Satisficed / Denied / Weakly Satisficed / Conflict). Wrong semantics. NFR is built for "softgoals" — quality attributes like security, performance, usability — where there is no Boolean satisfied/violated, only degrees. Manifold's verdict is Boolean. NFR's machinery is strictly more than we need today; we keep it in reserve.
Proof theory (Prawitz, Gentzen) Natural deduction: proofs decompose into sub-proofs. Wrong substrate. Proofs are about truth of mathematical propositions; manifold's nodes are specs of artifacts to be built. Also: while formal proofs are trees, real proof assistants (Coq, Lean, ACL2) use DAGs via hash-consing — making the tree a theoretical fiction in practice.
KAOS (van Lamsweerde et al.) Goal-oriented requirements engineering: business intent → refinement DAG → operational specs with Boolean satisfaction. Matches manifold's primitive verbatim. AND/OR DAG, Boolean satisfaction, layered intent, cycles forbidden, multi-parent supported, coverage rule. The KAOS toolkit has extras we don't need yet (obstacles, agents, operations) — but they're additive, not blocking.

Bottom line: AND-OR graphs are the algorithmic relative; refinement calculus is a structural neighbor; KAOS is the actual home. The Boolean-satisfaction case with layered intent and AND/OR refinement is what KAOS was built to formalize.

§5 The full KAOS toolkit — and what manifold uses

KAOS is not just the AND/OR DAG. It is a full methodology with multiple companion concepts. Here is what's in the toolkit and what manifold currently uses.

Manifold uses today

  • Goals (manifold calls them nodes)
  • AND-refinement (the contract rule)
  • Coverage rule (every non-leaf has ≥1 child)
  • Cycle prohibition (DAG, acyclic)
  • Layered abstraction (project-defined layers)
  • Constraint goals (manifold's kind: constraint = KAOS "constraint goals")

KAOS has it, manifold doesn't (yet)

  • OR-refinement — "any of these alternatives"
  • Obstacles — first-class "what could prevent this goal"
  • Refinement patterns — catalog of proven-sound decomposition templates
  • Conflict detection — formal check for "these two goals can't both hold"
  • LTL semantics — temporal operators on system traces
  • Agents — explicit assignment of responsibility

Why manifold deliberately leaves things behind

LTL semantics: KAOS's temporal logic is software-specific (it reasons about system execution traces). Manifold replaces it with pluggable verdict mechanisms (automated_check, python_assertion, human_signoff, llm_judge) so that non-software domains can have domain-appropriate verdicts.

Agents and operations: KAOS models WHO does WHAT. Manifold's orchestrator handles that at a different layer — manifold itself stays purely about what the spec says, not who realizes it how.

OR-refinement, obstacles, patterns, conflicts: Worth adding in future versions. Not blocking today. Each one is additive — adopting them doesn't require revisiting the substrate.

§6 What KAOS does NOT specify (limits and silences)

The question you asked specifically: does KAOS limit the number of layers? what should a leaf say? what should the root be? Here's the honest answer for each.

How many layers?

KAOS does not fix a layer count. The methodology says "refine until you reach operational goals" — goals an agent can satisfy without further decomposition. In published industrial KAOS specs, layer counts typically run from 3 to 7 depending on system complexity. Van Lamsweerde's textbook examples (medical patient monitoring, train signaling) tend to be 4–6 layers deep.

For manifold: no formal limit. But the discipline question — "do you stop because you've truly reached operational granularity, or because you've run out of patience" — is real, and manifold currently has no answer. Worth codifying a recommendation.

What should a leaf node be?

In KAOS: a leaf is an operational goal — one that can be assigned to an agent (human or software) who can satisfy it directly. The leaf is where intent meets execution. Operationally: a goal is a leaf when assigning it to an agent + an operation makes the goal hold.

For manifold: the equivalent is "the verdict mechanism resolves it without further sub-decomposition." A node with verdict.mechanism: automated_check + a working verdict.check command is operational. A node with verdict.mechanism: human_signoff + a clear acceptance criterion is operational. The leaf is where the verdict can fire.

This is a different criterion from "no further structural decomposition possible." A node could be structurally further decomposable but operationally adequate. KAOS allows this.

What should a root node be?

In KAOS: a strategic goal or business objective with no further parent — the highest-level intent driving the system. A spec may have multiple roots ("strategic objectives"); they are not required to be a single sentence. The root is the answer to "why does this system exist."

For manifold: a layer-0 node with empty parents:. Multiple roots are allowed (a project can have multiple top-level intentions). The root is the answer to "why does this project exist."

What KAOS is silent on (and manifold has to decide)

  • Render mode: KAOS doesn't say how to display the spec as a coherent narrative. Manifold's "book at different bolding levels" is a manifold invention.
  • Verdict mechanism implementation: KAOS uses LTL formulas; manifold uses shell-outs and LLM judges. Manifold's choice.
  • Multi-parent rendering convention: KAOS doesn't specify which parent is "primary." Manifold needs a rule (proposed: first entry in parents: is primary for narrative; others are cross-references).
  • Spec-time vs artifact-time verdicts: KAOS assumes spec-time only (the system isn't built yet). Manifold has both phases. Manifold's call to make.

§7 Does KAOS respect non-software domains?

This is the question that matters most given your "manifold should be domain-agnostic" commitment. The answer is yes, structurally — but only if you replace LTL with domain-appropriate verdict semantics. Here's the analysis.

The structural skeleton is universal

Strip KAOS down to its load-bearing parts:

  1. A set of goals (things to be true, achieved, maintained, or avoided).
  2. A refinement relation that decomposes parents into children, forming an AND/OR DAG.
  3. A satisfaction predicate for each leaf, returning Boolean (or — in NFR's extension — a qualitative label).
  4. A propagation rule: parent's satisfaction is computed from children's via ∧ (AND) or ∨ (OR).

None of these four parts is software-specific. They are formal-logical primitives. They apply unchanged to any domain where you have intentions, can decompose them, and can evaluate the leaves.

What IS software-specific in KAOS — and how to replace it

LTL operators

KAOS expresses goals in linear temporal logic (Achieve, Maintain, Avoid, Cease). These reason about system execution traces over time.

Replace with: manifold's pluggable verdict mechanism. Per-domain mechanisms: automated_check for software, llm_judge for prose, statistical_threshold for science, human_signoff universally.

Agent / operation model

KAOS assigns leaf goals to agents (human / system) and pairs each leaf with an operation.

Replace with: manifold's orchestrator owns "who realizes what." Manifold itself stays agent-agnostic. The spec describes the WHAT, not the WHO.

Industrial vocabulary

KAOS papers use terms like "satisfaction," "obstacle," "agent," "operationalization" — all software-flavored.

Replace with: manifold's vocabulary is project-defined per spec. Layer names (theme/act/scene, thesis/claim/evidence, intent/capabilities/contracts) live in spec.yaml, not in the framework.

Worked sketch: the same structural skeleton, three different domains

Software

Root: "Users can buy concert tickets"

Mid: "Search by date works" ∧ "Payment processes"

Leaf: "POST /checkout returns 200 with valid card"

Verdict: automated_check shell-out, exit 0 = satisfied

Novel

Root: "The map precedes the territory"

Mid: "Leonor inherits the archive" ∧ "Journey inward"

Leaf: "Scene: she finds her own handwriting on the impossible map"

Verdict: llm_judge — does the scene contain a definitive threshold-crossing?

Research paper

Root: "SAEs learn human-interpretable features"

Mid: "Latents align with concepts" ∧ "Ablation has predictable effect"

Leaf: "Probing accuracy > 85% on category labels"

Verdict: statistical_threshold — script returns float; compare to threshold

The structural skeleton is identical across all three. Only the verdict mechanism changes. This is what "domain-agnostic" actually means: a framework with the same topological rules and a pluggable evaluation layer.

§9 Open questions you still have to answer

Q1 — Adopt OR-refinement now, or defer?

KAOS has both. Manifold currently only has AND. OR is useful when a goal has multiple acceptable strategies (e.g. "authenticate user" = "password" OR "passkey" OR "OAuth"). Today manifold forces you to either pick one or make all three children (which then requires all three to be satisfied, which is wrong).

Recommendation: defer. It's additive — the existing AND-only spec stays valid when OR is added later. But if you have a real use case in the next month, fold it in.

Q2 — Adopt obstacle analysis?

KAOS treats "what could prevent this goal from being satisfied" as first-class structure with its own refinement DAG. Defensive design. Currently manifold has no analogue.

Recommendation: defer. Not blocking today. Worth revisiting when manifold is used on safety-critical specs.

Q3 — Codify a "leaf-hood" recommendation?

KAOS's answer is "agent + operation can satisfy it directly." Manifold's analogue is "verdict mechanism resolves it." But manifold doesn't currently SAY this in its design notes. A spec author can keep decomposing past the point where it's useful.

Recommendation: document the heuristic — "a node is a leaf when its verdict mechanism, configured, can fire and return a result without further decomposition." Doesn't require code changes; just an authoring discipline note.

Q4 — Adopt refinement patterns as "starter templates"?

KAOS has a catalog of proven-sound decomposition patterns (milestone-driven, case-driven, guard-introduction, divide-and-conquer). Manifold could ship these as templates that authors can apply when decomposing a goal.

Recommendation: worth a longer think later. Templates are valuable but should be domain-flavored (software templates, novel templates, paper templates). Not a v0.4 priority.

Q5 — Rename anything?

Manifold's current vocabulary (intent / capabilities / contracts / realizations) is project-defined per spec, but the framework also uses internal terms (node, layer, parent, verdict). KAOS's vocabulary (goal, refinement, satisfaction, agent) is more established. Should manifold align?

Recommendation: probably no. Manifold's terms are clearer for non-RE audiences. The KAOS citation in docs is enough to establish lineage. Keep "node" and "verdict" — they're more domain-agnostic than "goal" and "satisfaction."
